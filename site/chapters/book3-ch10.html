Book III — Ceremonial Magic — Chapter 10

Of the Three Names of God: El, Elohim, and Tetragrammaton

nominibus illis praesidentibus eorundemque interpretatione
Largely New

Perrone Compagni — Table of Comparison

1533 edition (3:10): new, except for two short passages from W, 3: 16 and the list of ten Names of God from W, 3: 50

This chapter, nominibus illis praesidentibus eorundemque interpretatione (“Of the Three Names of God: El, Elohim, and Tetragrammaton”), occupies a significant place in Agrippa's systematic exposition of occult philosophy. In the 1533 Cologne printed edition (De occulta philosophia libri tres, sigla K in Perrone Compagni's critical apparatus), the text represents Agrippa's mature formulation of the ideas it addresses, the product of over two decades of reading, travel, and revision since the composition of the Würzburg draft.

According to Perrone Compagni's table of comparison, this chapter is new in the 1533 edition and has no direct counterpart in the Würzburg manuscript (W = MS M.ch.q.50). The description notes: \u201cexcept for two short passages from W, 3: 16 and the list of ten Names of God from W, 3: 50.\u201d Its addition reflects the conceptual enrichment Agrippa undertook between approximately 1510 and the early 1530s, incorporating sources and arguments unavailable or unexplored in the juvenile draft.

The stability of this chapter across the manuscript and printed versions suggests it belongs to the secure core of Agrippa's natural-magical argument as it was constituted by 1510. Such chapters allow scholars to reconstruct the intellectual foundation on which the more elaborate structures of the 1533 edition were built.

Scholarly Perspectives

The divine-name chapters are the Kabbalistic apex of DOP's ascending programme. Lehrich (2003) reads them as Agrippa's most serious engagement with the question of how human language can approach divine reality: the divine names are the points at which the infinite descends into the finite, and the magician who knows them participates in the divine creative act. Miles (2008) provides the crucial qualification: the names work only through the faith of those who use them, not through any intrinsic linguistic property — a position Agrippa states explicitly in III:11 but which structures the entire Kabbalistic section.

Perrone Compagni — Table of Comparison

1533 edition (1:34): W, 1:26

This chapter, fluxus atque virtutes corporum coelestium (“How We May Know What Things Are Under the Sun, Moon, or Stars”), occupies a significant place in Agrippa's systematic exposition of occult philosophy. In the 1533 Cologne printed edition (De occulta philosophia libri tres, sigla K in Perrone Compagni's critical apparatus), the text represents Agrippa's mature formulation of the ideas it addresses, the product of over two decades of reading, travel, and revision since the composition of the Würzburg draft.

This chapter corresponds closely to W 1:26 in the Würzburg draft and appears to be substantially preserved in the 1533 edition. The direct correspondence shows that Agrippa regarded this material as settled relatively early and did not feel compelled to revise it significantly.

The stability of this chapter across the manuscript and printed versions suggests it belongs to the secure core of Agrippa's natural-magical argument as it was constituted by 1510. Such chapters allow scholars to reconstruct the intellectual foundation on which the more elaborate structures of the 1533 edition were built.

Scholarly Perspectives

The stellar and planetary image chapters represent the practical heart of natural magic. Lehrich (2003) analyses the tradition of astrological talismans as drawing on the Arabic Picatrix and Ficino's De vita coelitus comparanda, both of which Agrippa incorporates into his tripartite cosmological framework. The images are not illustrations but operative instruments: by reproducing celestial forms in material media at astrologically propitious moments, the magician draws down specific planetary virtues.

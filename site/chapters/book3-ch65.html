This chapter, Operis totius conclusio (“Epilogue of the Whole Work”), occupies a significant place in Agrippa's systematic exposition of occult philosophy. In the 1533 Cologne printed edition (De occulta philosophia libri tres, sigla K in Perrone Compagni's critical apparatus), the text represents Agrippa's mature formulation of the ideas it addresses, the product of over two decades of reading, travel, and revision since the composition of the Würzburg draft.

Perrone Compagni's table of comparison indicates: “W, Epilog. + a substantial addition at the end (title not recorded in the Tables of Contents attached to A).” This relationship between the 1510 Würzburg draft and the 1533 printed edition reflects the complex evolution of Agrippa's thought across two decades.

The stability of this chapter across the manuscript and printed versions suggests it belongs to the secure core of Agrippa's natural-magical argument as it was constituted by 1510. Such chapters allow scholars to reconstruct the intellectual foundation on which the more elaborate structures of the 1533 edition were built.

Scholarly Perspectives

The epilogue completes DOP with a famous passage of apparent self-retraction, in which Agrippa distances himself from the very material he has compiled. Miles (2008) reads this retraction as deliberately paradoxical: because the retraction is included within the published text, it inevitably retracts itself, instantiating 'a typical Epimenidian self-referential loop of infinite flip-flopping.' The retraction is not a genuine disavowal but a rhetorical performance of the limits of language that the entire work has been exploring. Bowen (1972) compares the move to Erasmus's Praise of Folly and the tradition of paradoxical encomium: Agrippa both affirms and undercuts his own authority as a way of demonstrating that no single voice can contain truth.

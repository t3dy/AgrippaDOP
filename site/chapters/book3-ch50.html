Perrone Compagni — Table of Comparison

1533 edition (3:50): W, 3: 37 + three additions

This chapter, tur et quae agonizantibus contingunt (“How We Are to Understand the Presence of Divine Goodness”), occupies a significant place in Agrippa's systematic exposition of occult philosophy. In the 1533 Cologne printed edition (De occulta philosophia libri tres, sigla K in Perrone Compagni's critical apparatus), the text represents Agrippa's mature formulation of the ideas it addresses, the product of over two decades of reading, travel, and revision since the composition of the Würzburg draft.

This chapter corresponds to W 3:37 in the Würzburg draft, with shorter additions in the 1533 text. The core argument is preserved from W, but Agrippa refined it with supplementary passages, qualifying or extending particular points.

The extent of revision in this chapter illustrates the dynamic character of Agrippa's rewriting process. Rather than simply updating facts, he appears to have substantially reconfigured the argument. Comparison with the partial edition A — which represents an intermediate stage — may reveal whether the additions were introduced gradually or in a single later revision.

Scholarly Perspectives

The divine-name chapters are the Kabbalistic apex of DOP's ascending programme. Lehrich (2003) reads them as Agrippa's most serious engagement with the question of how human language can approach divine reality: the divine names are the points at which the infinite descends into the finite, and the magician who knows them participates in the divine creative act. Miles (2008) provides the crucial qualification: the names work only through the faith of those who use them, not through any intrinsic linguistic property — a position Agrippa states explicitly in III:11 but which structures the entire Kabbalistic section.

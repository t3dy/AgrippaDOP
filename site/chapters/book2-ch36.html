This chapter, terreni obediunt Scorpioni coelesti, sed etiam imagini Scorpionis illius, quae fuerit opportune sub eius ascensu ac dominio figurata. De imaginibus Zodiaci quas insculptae a suis stellis concipiant virtutes (“Of the Images of the Planets According to the Faces”), occupies a significant place in Agrippa's systematic exposition of occult philosophy. In the 1533 Cologne printed edition (De occulta philosophia libri tres, sigla K in Perrone Compagni's critical apparatus), the text represents Agrippa's mature formulation of the ideas it addresses, the product of over two decades of reading, travel, and revision since the composition of the Würzburg draft.

This chapter corresponds closely to W 2:5 in the Würzburg draft and appears to be substantially preserved in the 1533 edition. The direct correspondence shows that Agrippa regarded this material as settled relatively early and did not feel compelled to revise it significantly.

The stability of this chapter across the manuscript and printed versions suggests it belongs to the secure core of Agrippa's natural-magical argument as it was constituted by 1510. Such chapters allow scholars to reconstruct the intellectual foundation on which the more elaborate structures of the 1533 edition were built.

Scholarly Perspectives

The 'faces' or decans of the planets extend the talisman-making programme of the magic squares. Lehrich (2003) situates these image-chapters within the tradition of Arabic astrological magic (the Picatrix / Ghāyat al-Ḥakīm) and Ficino's De vita coelitus comparanda, both of which Agrippa systematises and integrates into his tripartite cosmological framework.

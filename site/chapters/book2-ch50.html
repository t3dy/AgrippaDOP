This chapter, tiones vero ad huiusmodi imagines et consimiles observandas docet nos ea pars astrologiae, quae de electionibus inscripta est. De observationibus quibusdam coelestibus et practica quarundam eiusdem (“Of Images and Seals of the Planets”), occupies a significant place in Agrippa's systematic exposition of occult philosophy. In the 1533 Cologne printed edition (De occulta philosophia libri tres, sigla K in Perrone Compagni's critical apparatus), the text represents Agrippa's mature formulation of the ideas it addresses, the product of over two decades of reading, travel, and revision since the composition of the Würzburg draft.

Based on W 2:16 of the Würzburg draft, this chapter was significantly augmented. Perrone Compagni identifies one or more substantial additions, indicating that Agrippa supplemented the original argument with new material, possibly drawn from sources encountered after the 1510 composition.

The extent of revision in this chapter illustrates the dynamic character of Agrippa's rewriting process. Rather than simply updating facts, he appears to have substantially reconfigured the argument. Comparison with the partial edition A — which represents an intermediate stage — may reveal whether the additions were introduced gradually or in a single later revision.

Scholarly Perspectives

Lehrich (2003) treats De occulta philosophia II:50, 'Of certain celestial observations and the practice of some images,' as one of the two pivotal practical exempla of mathematical magic in Book II, and reads its tripartite structure as the moment where his broader argument about the operative magus receives its fullest demonstration. Applying Panofsky's tripartite distinction of pre-iconography, iconography, and iconology, Lehrich argues that the magus-constructor does not merely interpret an image but predetermines its reference structure, thereby controlling the iconological content and fixing a sign that carries indexical power over the world. The chapter's climactic passage — that only the one who, 'the elements being restrained, nature being overcome, the heavens being overpowered,' transcends the progress of angels and comes to the Archetype itself can 'give a soul to an image' — is for Lehrich the crux of his semiotic reading: mathematical magic is about enacting rather than merely understanding the celestial hierarchy. He further observes that this passage connects II:50 forward to Book III's ceremonial magic, revealing that all three varieties of Agrippan magic share the same telos of divine cooperation, the magus becoming what the text calls 'a cooperator' of the Archetype. The chapter thus occupies a hinge position in the historiographical debate about whether Agrippa's system is primarily theoretical or operational: Lehrich's reading insists that II:50 is precisely where the speculative architecture resolves into a practical and theurgic programme continuous with the angelic and divine magic of Book III.
